Integers

What are Integers?

Real-world connection: Have you ever seen a weather report showing −5°C? Or pressed “B1” in a lift to go to the basement? Or heard someone say “I owe Rs 200”? All of these use negative numbers — and that’s exactly what integers are about!
  • You already know Natural Numbers: 1, 2, 3, 4, … (the counting numbers).
  • Add zero and you get Whole Numbers: 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, …
  • Now extend in the other direction and you get Integers: … −3, −2, −1, 0, 1, 2, 3, …
  • Integers include all positive numbers, zero, and negative numbers. They go on forever in both directions!
  • Zero is special — it is neither positive nor negative. It sits right in the middle.
Where do we see negative numbers in real life?
• Temperature: −10°C means 10 degrees below zero (very cold!)
• Buildings: Floor B2 (basement 2) is 2 floors below ground level = −2
• Money: If you owe Rs 500, your balance is −500
• Sea level: The Dead Sea is 430 m below sea level = −430 m
• Timeline: 200 BC means 200 years before year 0

Comparing Integers

  • On a number line, the number further to the right is always bigger.
  • Every positive number is greater than zero, and every negative number is less than zero.
  • Every positive number is greater than every negative number (e.g., 1 > −1000).
Tricky part — comparing two negative numbers:
Many students think −8 is bigger than −3 because 8 > 3. But that’s wrong! Think of temperature: −8°C is colder than −3°C. Colder means smaller. On the number line, −8 is further LEFT than −3, so −8 < −3.

Rule: For negative numbers, the one with the bigger digit is actually smaller! −100 < −1.

Addition of Integers

There are two cases when adding integers:

Case 1: Same signs — Add and keep the sign

  • Positive + Positive: (+5) + (+3) = +8. This is just normal addition.
  • Negative + Negative: (−5) + (−3) = −8. You’re going further into the negatives.
Money analogy: You owe Rs 5 (that’s −5). Then you borrow Rs 3 more (that’s −3). Now you owe Rs 8 total (−8). Debts add up!

Case 2: Different signs — Subtract and take the sign of the bigger number

  • (−7) + (+4): Subtract 7 − 4 = 3. Since 7 > 4 and 7 is negative, the answer is −3.
  • (+9) + (−4): Subtract 9 − 4 = 5. Since 9 > 4 and 9 is positive, the answer is +5.
Money analogy: You owe Rs 7 (−7). You earn Rs 4 (+4). You pay off part of the debt, but you still owe Rs 3 (−3). The debt was bigger, so the result stays negative.
Number line rule: Adding a positive number = move right. Adding a negative number = move left. The sign tells you the direction!

Subtraction of Integers

  • The most important rule: Subtracting a number = Adding its opposite.
  • In math language: a − b = a + (−b). And a − (−b) = a + b.
But WHY does subtracting a negative = adding?

Think of it this way: A “negative” is like a debt. If someone removes (subtracts) your debt (negative), that’s the same as giving you money (positive)!

• You owe Rs 300 to a friend (−300).
• Your friend says “forget it, I’m removing that debt”. That’s subtracting a negative.
• Result: you gained Rs 300! Subtracting (−300) = Adding (+300).

More examples to make it click:

  • (−12) − (−5) = (−12) + 5 = −7 (you owed 12, someone forgave 5, you still owe 7)
  • (−4) − 7 = (−4) + (−7) = −11 (you owed 4, then borrowed 7 more)
  • 0 − (−6) = 0 + 6 = 6 (removing a debt from zero = a gain)

The Sign Rules Summary

What about multiple negatives?
Count the number of negative signs:
• Even number of negatives = positive result (they cancel in pairs)
• Odd number of negatives = negative result
Example: (−2) × (−3) × (−4) = three negatives (odd) = −24

Division of Integers

  • Division follows the exact same sign rules as multiplication.
  • Same signs → positive: (−12) ÷ (−4) = +3
  • Different signs → negative: (−15) ÷ 3 = −5
  • Division is the reverse of multiplication. If (−3) × 4 = −12, then (−12) ÷ 4 = −3.
Important! You can NEVER divide by zero. (−7) ÷ 0 is undefined (not zero, not any number — it simply doesn’t exist). Think about it: there’s no number you can multiply by 0 to get −7.

Properties of Integers

1. Closure Property

  • Integers are closed under addition, subtraction, and multiplication. This means: integer + integer = always an integer.
  • Example: (−3) + 5 = 2 (integer). (−4) × 3 = −12 (integer).
  • But NOT closed under division: (−7) ÷ 2 = −3.5 (not an integer!).

2. Commutative Property

  • Addition: a + b = b + a. Example: 3 + (−5) = (−5) + 3 = −2
  • Multiplication: a × b = b × a. Example: (−4) × 7 = 7 × (−4) = −28
  • NOT for subtraction: 5 − 3 = 2, but 3 − 5 = −2. They’re different!

3. Associative Property

  • Addition: (a + b) + c = a + (b + c). Example: (2 + 3) + 4 = 2 + (3 + 4) = 9.
  • Multiplication: (a × b) × c = a × (b × c).
  • NOT for subtraction: (10 − 5) − 2 = 3, but 10 − (5 − 2) = 7. Different!

4. Distributive Property

a × (b + c) = a × b + a × c — you can “distribute” the multiplication over addition.

5. Identity Elements

  • Additive identity (0): a + 0 = a. Adding zero changes nothing. Example: −7 + 0 = −7.
  • Multiplicative identity (1): a × 1 = a. Multiplying by 1 changes nothing. Example: −7 × 1 = −7.

6. Additive Inverse

  • Every integer has an opposite that adds up to zero: a + (−a) = 0.
  • The opposite of 5 is −5 (because 5 + (−5) = 0). The opposite of −3 is 3.

Key Formulas

a − (−b) = a + b — Subtracting a negative = adding.
(−a) × (−b) = ab — Negative × negative = positive.
(−a) × b = −(ab) — Negative × positive = negative.
a ÷ (−b) = −(a ÷ b) — Dividing by a negative gives a negative.
a + (−a) = 0 — Every integer plus its opposite equals zero.

Worked Examples

Example 1: Find (−3) × (−5) × 2

Step 1: (−3) × (−5) = +15 (negative × negative = positive, using the pattern rule we learned).
Step 2: 15 × 2 = 30 (positive × positive = positive).
Check: We had 2 negative signs (even count), so the final answer must be positive. 30 is positive. ✓

Example 2: Simplify (−36) ÷ (−4)

Step 1: Both numbers are negative → same signs → result is positive.
Step 2: 36 ÷ 4 = 9. So the answer is +9.
Verify: (−4) × 9 = −36 ✓ (division is the reverse of multiplication).

Example 3: Evaluate (−8) + (−3) − (−5)

Step 1: Convert subtraction to addition: (−8) + (−3) + 5.
Step 2: Add the negatives first: (−8) + (−3) = −11 (same signs, add and keep the sign).
Step 3: −11 + 5 = −6 (different signs, subtract: 11 − 5 = 6, bigger number is negative, so answer is −6).

Example 4: Submarine Problem

A submarine is at a depth of −150 m (150 m below sea level). It rises 80 m, then dives 45 m. What is its final depth?

Step 1: Start at −150 m.
Step 2: Rises 80 m = adding +80: −150 + 80 = −70 m.
Step 3: Dives 45 m = adding −45: −70 + (−45) = −115 m.
Answer: The submarine is at 115 m below sea level.

Example 5: Verify the Distributive Property with Negatives

Show that (−2) × (5 + (−3)) = (−2) × 5 + (−2) × (−3)

LHS: (−2) × (5 + (−3)) = (−2) × 2 = −4
RHS: (−2) × 5 + (−2) × (−3) = −10 + 6 = −4
LHS = RHS = −4 ✓ The distributive property works even with negatives!

Practice Problems

Easy

1. Find the value of (−7) + 3.

Show Answer
(−7) + 3 = −4. Different signs: 7 − 3 = 4, and 7 is bigger and negative, so result is −4.
Easy

2. Find the value of (−4) × 5.

Show Answer
(−4) × 5 = −20. Different signs (negative × positive) = negative.
Medium

3. Find the value of (−15) ÷ 3 + (−2) × 4.

Show Answer
First: (−15) ÷ 3 = −5. Then: (−2) × 4 = −8. Finally: −5 + (−8) = −13.
Medium

4. Arrange in ascending order: −3, 5, −8, 2.

Show Answer
From smallest to largest: −8, −3, 2, 5. (Think of temperature: −8°C is coldest.)
Hard

5. Find: (−2) × (−3) × (−4) × (−1).

Show Answer
4 negative signs (even count) → positive result. 2 × 3 × 4 × 1 = 24. Answer: +24.
Hard

6. The temperature was −8°C in the morning. It rose 15°C during the day, then dropped 6°C at night. What was the final temperature?

Show Answer
−8 + 15 = 7°C. Then 7 − 6 = 1°C.
Easy

7. Find (−9) + 4.

Show Answer
Different signs: 9 − 4 = 5, bigger number (9) is negative, so answer is −5.
Easy

8. What is (−6) × 1?

Show Answer
−6. Multiplying by 1 (the multiplicative identity) gives the same number.
Medium

9. Simplify: (−12) ÷ 4 + (−3) × 2.

Show Answer
(−12) ÷ 4 = −3. (−3) × 2 = −6. −3 + (−6) = −9.
Medium

10. Find: (−5) × 3 × (−2).

Show Answer
(−5) × 3 = −15. Then (−15) × (−2) = +30. Answer: 30. (2 negatives = positive.)
Hard

11. The temperature at midnight was −5°C. By noon it rose 12°C, then dropped 8°C by evening. What was the evening temperature?

Show Answer
−5 + 12 = 7°C. Then 7 − 8 = −1°C.
Hard

12. Evaluate: [(−36) ÷ 6] × [(−8) + 3].

Show Answer
First bracket: (−36) ÷ 6 = −6. Second bracket: (−8) + 3 = −5. Multiply: (−6) × (−5) = 30. Same signs = positive.

Common Mistakes

  • Thinking negative × negative = negative.
    (−3) × (−5) = −15
    (−3) × (−5) = +15 (same signs = positive!)
  • Forgetting to flip when subtracting a negative.
    5 − (−3) = 2
    5 − (−3) = 5 + 3 = 8
  • Thinking −8 is bigger than −3.
    −8 > −3
    −8 < −3 (further left on number line = smaller)
  • Dividing by zero.
    (−12) ÷ 0 = 0
    (−12) ÷ 0 is undefined (impossible!)
  • Thinking subtraction is commutative.
    5 − 3 = 3 − 5
    5 − 3 = 2, but 3 − 5 = −2 (they’re different!)
