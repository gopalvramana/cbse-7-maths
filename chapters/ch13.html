Chapter 13 · Algebra

Exponents and Powers

Summary

  • An exponent tells you how many times to multiply a number by itself. In an, a is the base, n is the exponent.
  • Example: 23 = 2 × 2 × 2 = 8.
  • Any number raised to the power 1 equals itself: a1 = a.
  • Any number raised to the power 0 equals 1: a0 = 1 (where a ≠ 0).
  • Multiplying same base: am × an = am+n (add the exponents).
  • Dividing same base: am ÷ an = am−n (subtract the exponents).
  • Power of a power: (am)n = am×n (multiply the exponents).
  • Standard form: expressing very large numbers using powers of 10 (e.g., 3,00,000 = 3 × 105).

Key Formulas (Laws of Exponents)

am × an = am+n
am ÷ an = am−n
(am)n = am×n
am × bm = (a × b)m
a0 = 1 (where a ≠ 0)

Worked Examples

Example 1: Simplifying using Laws

Simplify: 23 × 24

Step 1: Same base (2), so add exponents: 23+4 = 27
Step 2: 27 = 128. Answer: 128

Example 2: Division Law

Simplify: 56 ÷ 52

Step 1: Same base (5), subtract exponents: 56−2 = 54
Step 2: 54 = 625. Answer: 625

Example 3: Standard Form

Express 47,00,000 in standard form.

Step 1: Write as a number between 1 and 10 multiplied by a power of 10
Step 2: 47,00,000 = 4.7 × 106
Answer: 4.7 × 106

Common Mistakes

  • Multiplying the bases instead of adding the exponents (23 × 24 ≠ 47, it's 27!).
  • Thinking a0 = 0 — it's actually 1 (for any non-zero a).
  • Adding exponents when bases are different — the law only works for the SAME base.
  • Confusing (am)n (multiply exponents) with am × an (add exponents).
