Types of Triangles

Real-world connection: Look around you — the truss of a roof, the “play” button on your phone, a slice of pizza, road-warning signs — triangles are everywhere because they are the strongest shape in engineering!

Classification by Sides

  • Equilateral triangle: All 3 sides equal, all 3 angles = 60° (e.g., sides 5 cm, 5 cm, 5 cm).
  • Isosceles triangle: Exactly 2 sides equal, the angles opposite the equal sides are also equal (e.g., sides 5 cm, 5 cm, 3 cm).
  • Scalene triangle: No sides are equal, no angles are equal (e.g., sides 3 cm, 4 cm, 6 cm).

Classification by Angles

  • Acute triangle: All 3 angles are less than 90° (e.g., 60°, 70°, 50°).
  • Right triangle: Exactly one angle = 90° (e.g., 90°, 45°, 45°). The side opposite 90° is the hypotenuse.
  • Obtuse triangle: Exactly one angle is greater than 90° (e.g., 110°, 40°, 30°).
Exam tip: A triangle can never have more than one right angle or more than one obtuse angle. Why? Because two 90° angles already add up to 180°, leaving nothing for the third angle!

Angle Sum Property

∠A + ∠B + ∠C = 180° — The three interior angles of any triangle always add up to 180°.
WHY do the angles add to 180°? (Tear-and-arrange proof)

1. Draw any triangle on paper and label the three angles ∠1, ∠2, ∠3.
2. Tear off the three corners.
3. Arrange them so their vertices meet at a single point, side by side.
4. They form a straight line (180°)!

This works for every triangle you try — equilateral, scalene, obtuse, any triangle at all. The three angles always combine to make a straight angle.

Example 1: Two angles of a triangle are 45° and 65°. Find the third angle.

Step 1: Use the angle sum property: ∠A + ∠B + ∠C = 180°
Step 2: Substitute: 45° + 65° + ∠C = 180°
Step 3: 110° + ∠C = 180°
Step 4: ∠C = 180° − 110° = 70°

Example 2: The angles of a triangle are in the ratio 2:3:4. Find all three angles.

Step 1: Let the angles be 2x, 3x, and 4x.
Step 2: By angle sum property: 2x + 3x + 4x = 180°
Step 3: 9x = 180°, so x = 20°
Step 4: The angles are 2(20) = 40°, 3(20) = 60°, 4(20) = 80°
Check: 40 + 60 + 80 = 180° ✓

Example 3: One angle of a triangle is 90° and the other two are equal. Find them.

Step 1: Let each equal angle = x.
Step 2: 90° + x + x = 180°
Step 3: 2x = 90°, so x = 45°
Step 4: The triangle has angles 90°, 45°, 45° (a right isosceles triangle).

Exterior Angle Property

Exterior angle = Sum of the two remote (non-adjacent) interior angles

Example: An exterior angle of a triangle is 120°. One remote interior angle is 50°. Find the other.

Step 1: Exterior angle = sum of remote interior angles
Step 2: 120° = 50° + other angle
Step 3: Other angle = 120° − 50° = 70°
Step 4: The adjacent interior angle = 180° − 120° = 60°. Check: 50 + 70 + 60 = 180° ✓

Triangle Inequality Property

a + b > c,   b + c > a,   a + c > b
The sum of any two sides of a triangle must be greater than the third side. Check ALL three pairs!
Real-world example (Sticks puzzle): You have three sticks: 3 cm, 4 cm, and 8 cm. Can you form a triangle?

Check: 3 + 4 = 7. Is 7 > 8? No! 7 < 8.
Even though 3 + 8 = 11 > 4 and 4 + 8 = 12 > 3, just one failure is enough.
These sticks cannot form a triangle. The two shorter sticks would just lie flat along the longest one!

Example: Can sides 5 cm, 7 cm, and 10 cm form a triangle?

Step 1: Check pair 1: 5 + 7 = 12 > 10 ✓
Step 2: Check pair 2: 5 + 10 = 15 > 7 ✓
Step 3: Check pair 3: 7 + 10 = 17 > 5 ✓
Step 4: All three conditions pass. Yes, they form a triangle.
Exam tip — shortcut: You only really need to check if the sum of the two smaller sides is greater than the largest side. If that works, the other two pairs automatically work. But in exams, show all three checks for full marks!

Pythagoras Theorem

a² + b² = c² — In a right triangle, the square of the hypotenuse equals the sum of the squares of the other two sides.

Discovering Pythagorean Triples

Pattern discovery: Some sets of whole numbers perfectly satisfy a² + b² = c². These are called Pythagorean triples:

• 3, 4, 5:   3² + 4² = 9 + 16 = 25 = 5² ✓
• 5, 12, 13:   5² + 12² = 25 + 144 = 169 = 13² ✓
• 8, 15, 17:   8² + 15² = 64 + 225 = 289 = 17² ✓

Notice: any multiple of a triple is also a triple! 3,4,5 → 6,8,10 → 9,12,15 → ...
Real-world example (Ladder against a wall): A ladder 13 m long leans against a wall. Its foot is 5 m from the wall. How high up the wall does it reach?

The ladder, wall, and ground form a right triangle. Ladder = hypotenuse = 13 m. Base = 5 m.
Height² = 13² − 5² = 169 − 25 = 144. Height = √144 = 12 m.

Example 1: Find the hypotenuse of a right triangle with legs 6 cm and 8 cm.

Step 1: c² = a² + b² = 6² + 8²
Step 2: c² = 36 + 64 = 100
Step 3: c = √100 = 10 cm

Example 2: A right triangle has hypotenuse 25 cm and one leg 7 cm. Find the other leg.

Step 1: a² + b² = c², so a² = c² − b²
Step 2: a² = 25² − 7² = 625 − 49 = 576
Step 3: a = √576 = 24 cm
Check: 7² + 24² = 49 + 576 = 625 = 25² ✓

Median and Altitude

  • Median: A line segment joining a vertex to the midpoint of the opposite side. Every triangle has 3 medians, and they all meet at the centroid.
  • Altitude: A perpendicular line segment from a vertex to the opposite side (or its extension). Every triangle has 3 altitudes, and they all meet at the orthocentre.
  • In general, the median and altitude from the same vertex are different line segments — unless the triangle is isosceles (from the apex vertex) or equilateral.
Exam tip: “Median bisects the opposite side” (goes to midpoint). “Altitude is perpendicular to the opposite side” (makes a 90° angle). Do not confuse the two!

Exercise 6.1 — Angle Sum Property & Exterior Angles (8 Questions)

Q1. In ▵PQR, ∠P = 30° and ∠Q = 60°. Find ∠R.

Show Solution
Step 1: By the angle sum property: ∠P + ∠Q + ∠R = 180°
Step 2: 30° + 60° + ∠R = 180°
Step 3: ∠R = 180° − 90° = 90°
Step 4: So ▵PQR is a right triangle with the right angle at R.

Q2. The angles of a triangle are (2x + 5)°, (3x − 10)°, and (x + 5)°. Find all three angles.

Show Solution
Step 1: Angle sum property: (2x + 5) + (3x − 10) + (x + 5) = 180
Step 2: Combine like terms: 6x + 0 = 180
Step 3: 6x = 180, so x = 30
Step 4: Angles: 2(30)+5 = 65°, 3(30)−10 = 80°, 30+5 = 35°
Check: 65 + 80 + 35 = 180° ✓

Q3. One angle of a triangle is 80° and the other two angles are equal. Find them.

Show Solution
Step 1: Let each equal angle = x.
Step 2: 80° + x + x = 180°
Step 3: 2x = 100°, so x = 50°
Step 4: The three angles are 80°, 50°, 50°. This is an isosceles triangle.

Q4. Can a triangle have all angles less than 60°? Why or why not?

Show Solution
Step 1: Suppose all three angles are less than 60°.
Step 2: Then the sum of the three angles would be less than 60 + 60 + 60 = 180°.
Step 3: But the angle sum property says the sum must be exactly 180°.
Step 4: This is a contradiction. No, a triangle cannot have all angles less than 60°.

Q5. An exterior angle of a triangle is 110°. One of the remote interior angles is 40°. Find all angles of the triangle.

Show Solution
Step 1: Exterior angle = sum of remote interior angles.
Step 2: 110° = 40° + second remote interior angle
Step 3: Second remote interior angle = 110° − 40° = 70°
Step 4: The adjacent interior angle = 180° − 110° = 70°
Step 5: All angles: 40°, 70°, 70°. Check: 40 + 70 + 70 = 180° ✓

Q6. An exterior angle of a triangle is 130° and the two remote interior angles are equal. Find all angles.

Show Solution
Step 1: Let each remote interior angle = x.
Step 2: Exterior angle = x + x = 2x
Step 3: 2x = 130°, so x = 65°
Step 4: Adjacent interior angle = 180° − 130° = 50°
Step 5: All angles: 65°, 65°, 50°. Check: 65 + 65 + 50 = 180° ✓

Q7. Find the value of x in a triangle where the angles are x°, 2x°, and 3x°.

Show Solution
Step 1: Angle sum: x + 2x + 3x = 180°
Step 2: 6x = 180°
Step 3: x = 30°
Step 4: The angles are 30°, 60°, 90°. This is a right triangle!

Q8. In ▵ABC, ∠A = 50° and the exterior angle at B is 125°. Find ∠B and ∠C.

Show Solution
Step 1: The interior angle at B and the exterior angle at B form a linear pair.
Step 2: ∠B = 180° − 125° = 55°
Step 3: By angle sum property: ∠C = 180° − 50° − 55° = 75°
Verify with exterior angle property: Ext ∠B = ∠A + ∠C = 50 + 75 = 125° ✓
Exercise 6.2 — Triangle Inequality (5 Questions)

Q1. Is it possible to have a triangle with sides 2 cm, 3 cm, and 5 cm?

Show Solution
Step 1: Check: 2 + 3 = 5. Is 5 > 5? No! 5 is not greater than 5 (must be strictly greater).
Step 2: The triangle inequality fails.
Step 3: No, these sides cannot form a triangle. They would form a straight line (degenerate case).

Q2. Is it possible to have a triangle with sides 3 cm, 6 cm, and 7 cm?

Show Solution
Step 1: Check pair 1: 3 + 6 = 9 > 7 ✓
Step 2: Check pair 2: 3 + 7 = 10 > 6 ✓
Step 3: Check pair 3: 6 + 7 = 13 > 3 ✓
Step 4: All three conditions satisfied. Yes, they can form a triangle.

Q3. Is it possible to have a triangle with sides 6 cm, 3 cm, and 2 cm?

Show Solution
Step 1: Check the two smallest sides: 3 + 2 = 5. Is 5 > 6? No!
Step 2: The triangle inequality fails.
Step 3: No, these cannot form a triangle.

Q4. The two sides of a triangle are 5 cm and 9 cm. What are the possible lengths for the third side?

Show Solution
Step 1: Let the third side = x. By triangle inequality:
Step 2: 5 + x > 9 ⇒ x > 4
Step 3: 5 + 9 > x ⇒ x < 14
Step 4: So the third side must satisfy: 4 < x < 14
Step 5: The third side can be any length between 4 cm and 14 cm (not including 4 or 14).

Q5. Which of the following can be the sides of a triangle? (a) 10, 5, 6 (b) 1, 2, 4 (c) 8, 8, 8

Show Solution
(a) 10, 5, 6: 5 + 6 = 11 > 10 ✓, 5 + 10 = 15 > 6 ✓, 6 + 10 = 16 > 5 ✓. Yes!
(b) 1, 2, 4: 1 + 2 = 3. Is 3 > 4? No! Cannot form a triangle.
(c) 8, 8, 8: 8 + 8 = 16 > 8 ✓ (all pairs pass). Yes! (equilateral triangle)
Exercise 6.3 — Pythagoras Theorem (5 Questions)

Q1. Find the hypotenuse of a right triangle with legs 9 cm and 12 cm.

Show Solution
Step 1: Apply Pythagoras: c² = a² + b²
Step 2: c² = 9² + 12² = 81 + 144 = 225
Step 3: c = √225 = 15 cm
Note: 9, 12, 15 is a multiple of the 3, 4, 5 triple (each × 3).

Q2. A right triangle has hypotenuse 17 cm and one leg 8 cm. Find the other leg.

Show Solution
Step 1: a² + b² = c², so a² = c² − b²
Step 2: a² = 17² − 8² = 289 − 64 = 225
Step 3: a = √225 = 15 cm
Check: 8² + 15² = 64 + 225 = 289 = 17² ✓ (This is the 8, 15, 17 triple!)

Q3. Check whether a triangle with sides 5, 12, and 13 is a right triangle.

Show Solution
Step 1: The longest side is 13 (potential hypotenuse).
Step 2: Check: 5² + 12² = 25 + 144 = 169
Step 3: 13² = 169
Step 4: Since 5² + 12² = 13², yes, it is a right triangle.

Q4. A ladder 15 m long is placed against a wall. The foot is 9 m from the wall. How high does the ladder reach?

Show Solution
Step 1: The ladder, wall, and ground form a right triangle. Ladder = hypotenuse = 15 m. Base = 9 m.
Step 2: height² = 15² − 9² = 225 − 81 = 144
Step 3: height = √144 = 12 m
Check: 9² + 12² = 81 + 144 = 225 = 15² ✓

Q5. Is a triangle with sides 7, 24, and 25 a right triangle?

Show Solution
Step 1: The longest side is 25 (potential hypotenuse).
Step 2: Check: 7² + 24² = 49 + 576 = 625
Step 3: 25² = 625
Step 4: Since 7² + 24² = 25², yes, it is a right triangle.
Exercise 6.4 — Mixed Problems (6 Questions)

Q1. AM is a median of ▵ABC. If AB = 7 cm, BC = 10 cm, and AC = 9 cm, what is BM?

Show Solution
Step 1: A median from vertex A goes to the midpoint M of the opposite side BC.
Step 2: Since M is the midpoint of BC, BM = BC ÷ 2
Step 3: BM = 10 ÷ 2 = 5 cm

Q2. In a right triangle, the two legs are 6 cm and 8 cm. Find (a) the hypotenuse (b) the perimeter.

Show Solution
(a) Finding the hypotenuse:
Step 1: c² = 6² + 8² = 36 + 64 = 100
Step 2: c = √100 = 10 cm
(b) Finding the perimeter:
Step 3: Perimeter = 6 + 8 + 10 = 24 cm

Q3. The altitude from vertex A of ▵ABC to side BC is 8 cm and BC = 12 cm. If the altitude from B to AC is 10 cm, find AC.

Show Solution
Step 1: Area of a triangle = ½ × base × height.
Step 2: Using BC as base: Area = ½ × 12 × 8 = 48 cm²
Step 3: Using AC as base with altitude 10: 48 = ½ × AC × 10
Step 4: 48 = 5 × AC, so AC = 48 ÷ 5 = 9.6 cm

Q4. A triangle has sides in the ratio 3:4:5 and its perimeter is 60 cm. Find the sides and check if it is a right triangle.

Show Solution
Step 1: Let the sides be 3x, 4x, and 5x.
Step 2: Perimeter = 3x + 4x + 5x = 12x = 60, so x = 5.
Step 3: Sides: 3(5) = 15 cm, 4(5) = 20 cm, 5(5) = 25 cm
Step 4: Check Pythagoras: 15² + 20² = 225 + 400 = 625 = 25² ✓
Step 5: Yes, it is a right triangle (since 3:4:5 is a Pythagorean triple).

Q5. An isosceles triangle has equal sides of 13 cm each and a base of 10 cm. Find the altitude from the apex to the base.

Show Solution
Step 1: In an isosceles triangle, the altitude from the apex bisects the base.
Step 2: Half of base = 10 ÷ 2 = 5 cm.
Step 3: The altitude, half-base, and equal side form a right triangle: h² + 5² = 13²
Step 4: h² = 169 − 25 = 144
Step 5: h = √144 = 12 cm

Q6. A field is in the shape of a right triangle with legs 40 m and 30 m. Find the length of the fence needed to go around it.

Show Solution
Step 1: We need the perimeter, so first find the hypotenuse.
Step 2: c² = 40² + 30² = 1600 + 900 = 2500
Step 3: c = √2500 = 50 m
Step 4: Perimeter = 40 + 30 + 50 = 120 m
Step 5: The fence needed is 120 m.

HOTS — Higher Order Thinking Skills Advanced

HardHOTS

1. Can a triangle have two obtuse angles? Explain with reasoning.

Show Answer
No. An obtuse angle is greater than 90°. If a triangle had two obtuse angles, their sum alone would exceed 90 + 90 = 180°. But the angle sum of a triangle is exactly 180°, so there would be nothing left (or negative) for the third angle. A triangle can have at most one obtuse angle.
HardHOTS

2. Find a Pythagorean triple where the smallest number is 9. (Hint: use the formula m² − n², 2mn, m² + n² with m = 5, n = 4.)

Show Answer
With m = 5, n = 4: m² − n² = 25 − 16 = 9, 2mn = 2(5)(4) = 40, m² + n² = 25 + 16 = 41. The triple is 9, 40, 41. Verify: 9² + 40² = 81 + 1600 = 1681 = 41² ✓
HardHOTS

3. The perimeter of an isosceles triangle is 50 cm. If the base is 18 cm, find the equal sides and verify the triangle inequality.

Show Answer
Let equal sides = a. Perimeter: a + a + 18 = 50 → 2a = 32 → a = 16 cm. Triangle inequality check: 16 + 16 = 32 > 18 ✓, 16 + 18 = 34 > 16 ✓. All conditions satisfied. The sides are 16 cm, 16 cm, and 18 cm.

Common Mistakes & How to Avoid Them

  • Thinking the angle sum is 360°
    ∠A + ∠B + ∠C = 360°
    ∠A + ∠B + ∠C = 180°
    360° is for quadrilaterals. Triangles sum to 180°!
  • Not checking ALL three pairs for triangle inequality
    3, 4, 8: “3+4=7, and 8+3=11>4, so yes!”
    3 + 4 = 7, but 7 < 8. Fails!
    Even one failure means no triangle. Always check all three pairs.
  • Using Pythagoras on non-right triangles
    Triangle with sides 5, 6, 8: “5²+6²=61=8²?” (No, 64≠61)
    Pythagoras only works for right triangles. First confirm there is a 90° angle, then apply the theorem.
  • Confusing median with altitude
    “The altitude goes to the midpoint of the opposite side.”
    Median goes to the midpoint. Altitude is perpendicular to the opposite side. They are different (unless the triangle is isosceles from that vertex)!
  • Forgetting that the exterior angle is NOT an interior angle
    “Exterior angle + two remote angles = 180°”
    Exterior angle = sum of two remote interior angles. The exterior angle and its adjacent interior angle add to 180° (linear pair).
